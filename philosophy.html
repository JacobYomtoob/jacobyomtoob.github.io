Educational Philosophy

How I think about teaching, learning, and the formation of physician-educators in neurology.

Core Belief

My Educational Philosophy

My educational philosophy is rooted in the conviction that effective medical education bridges the gap between theoretical knowledge and the lived experience of clinical care. As a movement disorders neurologist and physician-educator, I have seen firsthand how the translation of complex neuroscience into actionable clinical reasoning shapes the doctors our patients ultimately meet.

I believe that learning is not a passive process of receiving information, but an active one of constructing meaning. The science of learning — as articulated in works like Make It Stick by Brown, Roediger, and McDaniel — has deeply informed how I design and deliver education. Two principles in particular guide my teaching: spaced repetition and frequent retrieval practice. Rather than concentrating learning into a single session, I build curricula that return to key concepts over time, allowing knowledge to consolidate and deepen. Rather than relying on passive re-reading or lecture alone, I incorporate regular knowledge checks and low-stakes assessments that require learners to actively retrieve information — a practice shown to strengthen long-term retention far more effectively than repeated exposure alone.

Critically, I believe these evidence-based strategies only work when the material itself is engaging. In neurology — where subtle phenomenological distinctions can direct the entire diagnostic and therapeutic path — I leverage video-based case presentations, real patient examples, and clinically grounded questions to make retrieval practice feel relevant rather than rote. The goal is not just knowledge that sticks, but knowledge that learners want to pursue.

Principles

Teaching Values

01

Spaced Repetition

Learning that is concentrated into a single session fades quickly. I design educational programs — like my quarterly movement phenomenology rounds — to return to core concepts at spaced intervals, reinforcing and building on prior sessions so that knowledge accumulates rather than evaporates.

02

Retrieval Practice & Knowledge Checks

Testing is not just a measurement tool — it is one of the most powerful learning tools we have. I incorporate frequent, low-stakes knowledge checks into my teaching to prompt active retrieval rather than passive review. This was central to my RITE review email series and informs how I structure case-based sessions.

03

Visual Learning in Neurology

Movement disorders are inherently visual. I prioritize video-based, phenomenology-focused learning — exemplified by Movement After Dark at Mass General Brigham and Movement Phenomenology Rounds at Northwestern — to sharpen pattern recognition in a way that keeps learners genuinely engaged and that textbooks alone cannot convey.

04

Learner-Centered Design

Effective curriculum begins with understanding what learners already know and where they struggle. My work on curriculum review committees and educational material development reflects a commitment to designing for the actual learner, not an idealized one.

05

Patient-Centered Purpose

Ultimately, education in medicine exists for the patient. Whether I am teaching a resident about HD psychosis, presenting on Parkinson's disease to a patient support group, or helping a care partner navigate Huntington's disease, the measure of success is whether it improves care.

06

Continuous Reflection

I am a learner myself. Participation in the McGaw Health Professions Educator Clinical Scholars Program has deepened my grounding in educational theory and sharpened my capacity to design, deliver, and evaluate educational interventions rigorously.
