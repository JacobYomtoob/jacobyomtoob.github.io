Core Belief

My Educational Philosophy

My educational philosophy is grounded in the belief that meaningful medical education is inseparable from patient care. At its core, teaching should always serve the ultimate goal of improving patient outcomes. For this reason, I view clinical experiences not as a supplement to learning, but as its foundation. The most impactful education occurs when learners engage directly with patients, applying knowledge in real time and developing clinical reasoning within authentic contexts.

As a movement disorders neurologist and educator, my approach has been shaped significantly by developing resident movement phenomenology rounds. These sessions emphasize direct observation, hands-on examination, and collaborative discussion at the bedside. Rather than presenting movement disorders as abstract categories, I focus on helping learners recognize and interpret phenomenology through real patients. This approach allows key concepts to emerge naturally within the context of patient-centered care and reinforces both diagnostic skills and clinical judgment.

I believe that learning is an active process that requires engagement, reflection, and application. Educational strategies such as spaced repetition and retrieval practice inform how I structure teaching, but they are most effective when paired with clinically meaningful content. I aim to create learning experiences that are both rigorous and relevant, where learners are challenged to think critically while remaining grounded in real-world patient care.

Equally important is the learning environment in which education occurs. I strive to foster a setting that is open, collaborative, and psychologically safe. Learners should feel comfortable asking questions, expressing uncertainty, and making mistakes. This type of environment allows for growth and prepares trainees to navigate the higher-stakes realities of independent patient care with confidence and humility.

My commitment to learner-centered education extends beyond physician training. In developing the Huntington’s disease care partner bootcamp series, I have prioritized designing education around the needs of the learners themselves. By using preference surveys to understand what care partners want to learn, I structure content that is responsive rather than prescriptive. This approach ensures that education remains relevant, practical, and empowering for those it is intended to serve.

Ultimately, my goal as an educator is to cultivate clinicians and learners who are thoughtful, adaptable, and deeply committed to patient-centered care. By integrating hands-on clinical experience, evidence-informed teaching strategies, and a supportive learning environment, I aim to create educational experiences that are both impactful and enduring.

Principles

Teaching Values

01

Patient-Centered Purpose

Medical education begins and ends with patient care. Every teaching interaction should ultimately improve how we care for patients. I prioritize clinical experiences that allow learners to engage directly with patients, apply knowledge in real time, and develop clinical reasoning grounded in meaningful, patient-centered contexts.

02

Experiential & Phenomenology-Based Learning

Hands-on experience is essential for learning neurology. Through resident movement phenomenology rounds, I emphasize direct observation, bedside examination, and collaborative interpretation of clinical findings. This approach helps learners build diagnostic skills by recognizing patterns in real patients rather than relying solely on abstract frameworks.

03

Psychological Safety & Collaborative Learning

Learning requires an environment where trainees feel comfortable asking questions, expressing uncertainty, and making mistakes. I aim to foster an open, low-pressure setting that encourages curiosity and growth, allowing learners to build confidence before facing the higher-stakes demands of independent patient care.

04

Learner-Centered Design

Effective education starts with understanding the learner. In developing the Huntington’s disease care partner bootcamp, I use preference surveys to identify what participants want to learn and design content accordingly. This approach ensures that education is responsive, relevant, and aligned with real-world needs.

05

Spaced Repetition & Retrieval Practice

Durable learning requires reinforcement over time and active engagement. I incorporate spaced repetition and low-stakes retrieval practice into my teaching to strengthen retention and deepen understanding, particularly when paired with clinically meaningful cases.

06

Continuous Reflection

I approach teaching as an evolving practice. Ongoing involvement in medical education training and curriculum development continues to shape how I design, deliver, and refine educational experiences to better serve learners and patients alike.
