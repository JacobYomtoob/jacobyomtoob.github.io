My Approach

Educational Philosophy

How I think about teaching, learning, and the formation of physician-educators in neurology.

Core Belief

The Why Behind My Teaching

"The best teaching doesn't just transfer knowledge — it models a way of thinking, a posture of curiosity, and a commitment to the patient in the room."
— Jacob Yomtoob, MD

My educational philosophy is rooted in the conviction that effective medical education bridges the gap between theoretical knowledge and the lived experience of clinical care. As a movement disorders neurologist and physician-educator, I have seen firsthand how the translation of complex neuroscience into actionable clinical reasoning shapes the doctors our patients ultimately meet.

I believe that learning is not a passive process of receiving information, but an active one of constructing meaning. In neurology, where subtle phenomenological distinctions can direct the entire diagnostic and therapeutic path, learners must be trained not only in what to see, but how to see — and more importantly, how to think through uncertainty.

Principles

Teaching Values

01

Visual Learning in Neurology

Movement disorders are inherently visual. I prioritize video-based, phenomenology-focused learning — exemplified by the Movement After Dark and Movement Phenomenology Rounds series I created — to sharpen pattern recognition that textbooks alone cannot convey.

02

Learner-Centered Design

Effective curriculum begins with understanding what learners already know and where they struggle. My work on curriculum review committees and educational material development reflects a commitment to designing for the actual learner, not an idealized one.

03

Scholarship as Teaching

Research and education are not parallel tracks but intertwined ones. Sharing findings — through grand rounds, national conferences, and peer-reviewed publications — models the scholarly mindset I hope to instill in the residents and students I teach.

04

Caring for the Whole Learner

I believe educators are responsible not just for the intellectual development of their learners but their professional identity formation. Mentorship, honest feedback, and modeling resilience in the face of clinical complexity are all acts of teaching.

05

Patient-Centered Purpose

Ultimately, education in medicine exists for the patient. Whether I am teaching a resident about HD psychosis or a care partner how to support a loved one with Huntington's disease, the measure of success is whether it improves care.

06

Continuous Reflection

I am a learner myself. Participation in the McGaw Health Professions Educator Clinical Scholars Program has deepened my grounding in educational theory and sharpened my capacity to design, deliver, and evaluate educational interventions rigorously.
